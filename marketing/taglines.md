# Taglines & hook bank

Reusable one-liners. Mix into posts, READMEs, talk titles, slide headers. Each is
defensible against the fact base in `PATTERN.md` §6.

## Primary thesis (the ownable claim)
- **Stop generating tools. Start engineering loops.**
- The future of AI agents isn't prompt engineering. It's loop engineering.

## Hooks (scroll-stoppers)
- Your AI writes a CLI once and freezes it. That's the bug.
- Most software is generated once and frozen. Tomorrow's improves itself.
- Everyone's optimizing prompts. The next leverage is the loop.
- A smarter model doesn't make a better tool. A loop does.
- "It works on my machine" → "it converged, and here's the report."

## One-line descriptions (the "what")
- Point it at any API or codebase; it builds an agent-native CLI, grades it
  against reality, and refactors until the grade stops climbing.
- A self-improving loop for the age of AI agents — grounded in reality, safe by
  construction.
- The controller, memory, and convergence policy that turn four tools into one
  closed feedback loop.

## Proof points (drop-in credibility)
- An independent referee grades it — 5 dimensions, A–F, against real payloads.
  Never the model admiring its own code.
- Stops at the first of: target grade, plateau, budget, or a safety block.
  It can't degrade into a worse tool than it started.
- Every accepted fix becomes a regression test. A solved problem never comes back.
- An unsafe tool never ships, no matter how high it scores.
- "Converged" is a claim, not a fact, until a human confirms it.
- 286 tests, Python 3.11–3.13. Loop dynamics proven before any live run.

## The dogfood angle (signature differentiator)
- We don't just preach loop engineering — even this launch is run as a loop we
  keep grading and improving.
- Self-improving software, marketed by a self-improving process.

## Engagement questions (provoke a comment)
- Which of your generated tools is quietly stuck at "good enough"?
- If a referee graded your last AI-built tool against reality, what grade would it get?
- What's the gap between "it exists" and "it's actually good" in your codebase?
